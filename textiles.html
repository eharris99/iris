Iris Apfel first went to Paris with her parents, Samuel and Sadye Barrel, who sourced glass, mirrors, and apparel for the family businesses. During her 68-year marriage to textile businessman Carl Apfel, she traveled all over Europe, Tunisia, Morocco, India, Turkey, China, and beyond. In Paris, she went to high end design showrooms like Lelievre and Edmond Petit. She scoured flea markets for old fabrics and textiles, then hired specialized looms to recreate them for nine White House administrations, as well as clients like Greta Garbo, Marjorie Merriweather Post, and Jackie Onassis. The average person can’t travel, shop, and subcontract on that level, so style like Iris's might seem unachievable for people with other jobs and responsibilities. But more opportunities have opened up for ordinary people to obtain fabrics, textiles, and upholstery once available only to the trade. Websites like 1stdibs, Walnut Wallpaper, and Decorator’s Best allow us all access to materials at prices closer to wholesale, without a decorator's markup. In New York, trade spaces like the Decoration and Design Building and the New York Design Center allow people access to formerly exclusionary spaces with a letter of reference from LA Design Concepts. Iris may have expensive tastes, but she also learned democratic values at Loehmann’s. If you do make it to Paris, be sure to check out the fabric showrooms and flea markets listed here. Iris spent many hours there, "educating the eye," in the words of Bill Cunningham.
